## 4. Scar Recovery Hypothesis

### Background
Previous experiments have primarily focused on the speed of recovery (Recovery Cost) and external stability (delta_v, kinetic energy). However, in complex systems, recovery of function does not necessarily mean preservation of internal structure.

Like scar tissue in biological healing, repair after damage often results in a functionally adequate but structurally altered state rather than true regeneration of the original configuration.

### Hypothesis
Residue facilitates functional recovery but tends to alter the internal geometric structure (Anti-Phase relations, Dispersion patterns, relational configuration) during the process. In other words, faster recovery enabled by higher Residue comes with a higher likelihood of structural deviation from the pre-damage state — a "scar-like" recovery.

### Experimental Evidence (as of June 2026)
The Geometry Fidelity experiment (Option 1) revealed the following pattern:

- Increasing Residue tends to improve external recovery metrics (smaller final |delta_v|).
- However, **Geometry Fidelity** (similarity of relational geometry before damage vs. after recovery) decreases.
- Trials with particularly fast recovery (low Recovery Steps) showed notably lower Geometry Fidelity compared to slower recovery trials (Pearson r ≈ +0.61 between Recovery Steps and Fidelity).

This suggests that the mechanism by which Residue accelerates recovery may simultaneously reduce the fidelity with which the original relational structure is preserved.

### Interpretation
- **Functional Recovery**: The system returns to a stable dynamic state after perturbation (what we have mainly measured so far).
- **Structural Recovery (True Regeneration)**: Recovery that preserves the pre-damage geometric relations and constraints as much as possible.
- **Scar Recovery**: Residue helps the system reach stability quickly, but the resulting stable state often has altered internal geometry compared to the original.

This framing aligns better with observed VGE behavior than a simple "hallucination" model and resonates with phenomena in LLMs (correct answer but altered reasoning path), biological healing, ecosystems, and organizations.

### Implications
- Lower Recovery Cost should not automatically be interpreted as improvement without checking structural fidelity.
- Future experiments should explicitly measure **Geometry Fidelity** and **Trajectory Divergence** alongside traditional recovery metrics.
- It is worth investigating whether repeated recovery cycles lead to cumulative structural change (accumulation of "scars").
